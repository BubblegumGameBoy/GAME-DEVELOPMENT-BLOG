Making Piano Typing: The Feeling of "Playing a Classic with Your Own Hands" (and Sneaky Home-Row Practice)

My new browser game, Piano Typing, is live. Falling tiles tell you which key to press next, and as you press at your own pace, classics like Mozart's Turkish March come to life. It's a mix of rhythm game and typing. Free, no sign-up, and playable right away on any PC with a keyboard.

What I wanted was the vivid feeling of "playing it yourself"

The thing I cared about most in this game was not settling for a fake "sounds like you played it" effect. A falling tile tells you the next key to press; press it and a real piano note sounds, and the melody steps forward one note at a time. It's not that the performance plays itself when you hit a button. I wanted the tangible sense that your own fingers are making the sound.

On top of that, chaining correct notes builds a combo that layers bass and drums, then strings, then a full orchestra onto the piano. What starts as a sparse solo piano grows, by your own hand, into a thick ensemble. That feeling of "I'm building this piece" is the real highlight.

And crucially, there's no timing judgment. The falling tiles are just a preview of the next key to press; you don't have to sync to their descent. In fact, dutifully waiting to match the tiles tends to throw you off the song's natural tempo. So I want you to play freely, at whatever rhythm feels right. You can't fail on a time-out either, so even someone who's never touched an instrument or a rhythm game can play a whole piece start to finish.

Three songs everyone knows

For this prototype release, I lined up three tunes you'll have heard before.

  • My Grandfather's Clock ... for practice. It's arranged in easy-to-play C major, so start here.
  • Für Elise (Beethoven)
  • Turkish March (Mozart: Rondo Alla Turca, K.331, 3rd movement)

The melodies aren't transcribed by ear; the pitches come from public-domain sheet-music data, so they follow the originals. I plan to add more songs as I see how people respond.

Before I knew it, it had become home-row practice material

This is something I only noticed after building it, but Piano Typing turned out to be a pretty solid "home-row practice tool" as well.

The game automatically assigns frequently used notes to the home position (left hand ASDF, right hand JKL;). The on-screen keyboard at the bottom is also color-coded per finger, telling you which finger to use each time, like "[Q] then left pinky." In other words, even if you don't know your do-re-mi, moving your fingers along with the guide naturally trains the motion of "pressing the right key with the right finger, without looking at your hands."

It wasn't material I set out to make. Chasing "I want to play a classic and feel good doing it" led, as a result, to basic typing practice. That was the discovery. The more someone struggles with typing, the more this can be a way to get comfortable with the home position while having fun. Since we're here, let me say a little about the home position itself.

What the home position actually is

The home position is the "base" where you rest your fingers on both hands. From index to pinky, the left hand sits on F, D, S, A, and the right hand on J, K, L, ; (semicolon). F and J have small bumps, so even without looking you can find them with your index fingers and always return to your home spot. Each finger reaches out the shortest way to its assigned key and comes straight back to base after pressing. That's the foundation of touch typing, "typing without looking at your hands."

Three ways it makes life easier once you learn it

  • You can type while watching the screen: once your fingers have fixed home spots, you no longer hunt for keys with your eyes. With your gaze locked on the screen, you can write while thinking, and react in games or chat without looking down.
  • Your input speed goes up: each finger reaches its key by the shortest path from home, so finger travel is minimal. The detour of "search, confirm, press" disappears, and what you think flows straight to your fingers.
  • You make fewer typos: pressing set keys with set fingers keeps your fingering stable and your fingers from hesitating. Fewer re-dos means you end up faster still, and it's easier on your wrists and shoulders too.

The logic is simple, but the home position is something you learn with your fingers, not your head. That's exactly why a game where your fingers move naturally as you play makes for good practice. Start with My Grandfather's Clock, moving your fingers along with the guide without looking at your hands.

Give it a try

Playing is easy. Click the game screen once, then just watch the tiles that show you the next key to press and press along at your own pace. There's no timing to match, so slow, one note at a time is perfectly fine. You can technically play on a phone by tapping the lit tiles, but a PC with a keyboard, where you can make use of the fingering guide, is what I'd recommend.

Start by playing a classic from beginning to end with your own hands in Piano Typing. And if you'd like to get used to the keystrokes themselves in other typing games, Popcorn Typing and Soldier Typing are both here for you.
